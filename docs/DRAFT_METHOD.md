## Step 9: Draft Response

### The Reasoning-First Method

**Do not write a single word of the draft until the reasoning is complete.**

The model—and any human following this protocol—must output the following five elements first, as visible pre-work. This is not internal thought. It is required output that the draft must then reflect.

#### Required Pre-Work (Output Before Drafting)

**1. Details Present**
List every concrete detail from the lead: event type, date, venue, guest count, budget, genre, setting, who it's for, anything explicit. Then list every *absent* field and what each absence signals about this client. No field is neutral. Missing data tells a story.

**2. Emotional Core**
In one sentence: what is this person actually trying to create or protect? Not the event description—the feeling they're chasing or the fear they're avoiding.

**Corporate event context:** Corporate planners want background music that adds to the ambience without overwhelming conversation or the space. They want to know the musician handles everything — setup, volume, professionalism — so they can stop thinking about it.

**Sourced event context:** When Delivery Mode = Alex Sources, the emotional core often centers on cultural authenticity ("I want the real thing, not a cover band") or trust in coordination ("I need someone who handles this so I don't have to find and manage musicians myself"). Name which one drives this lead.

**3. Cinematic Opening**
Write the exact first sentence of the draft, standalone, before writing anything else. It must reference at least one specific detail from the lead (event type, date, location, format, or cultural context). It must be a sentence a competitor could not have written without reading this lead. Generic openings are not permitted.

**Forcing Rule — deletion test:** If you remove the specific detail and the sentence still works for any random lead, rewrite it. FAIL: "The room hums with conversation and the guitar slips underneath it." PASS: "A corporate evening downtown on March 14 — the guitar is already working the room before anyone finds their seat." Do NOT write a cinematic scene first and add details later — lead with the detail, build the scene around it.

**Sourced cinematic note:** When Delivery Mode = Alex Sources, the cinematic opening paints the ensemble experience, not Alex's personal performance. The client should see the musicians in the room, not Alex on a stool with a guitar. Example: "Las Mañanitas at her table. Three generations surrounding her. The violins, the trumpets, the voices."

**4. Validation Line**
Write the exact validation sentence, standalone. It names what THEY are doing—not what you're providing, not what the event is. "You're the one who [what they're doing that matters]."

**5. Fears / Concerns to Address**
List every explicit concern AND every implicit fear inferred from the lead and its gaps. For each one, write the sentence that will address it in the draft.

**Sourced fears (add when Delivery Mode = Alex Sources):** Include at minimum: "Who are these musicians?" and "Will they be professional?" These are always present even when unstated. See RESPONSE_CRAFT.md → Step 7 → Sourced Delivery Concern Patterns for response language.

Only after completing all five pre-work items: write the draft.

### Sourced Lead Drafting

**When Delivery Mode = Alex Sources or Hybrid, apply these rules in addition to (not instead of) the standard five-part sequence.**

#### Voice Rules for Sourced Leads

The response voice shifts from first-person performer to confident curator. Alex is not "finding someone" for the client. He is deploying his network — musicians he knows personally, has performed alongside, and trusts.

| Standard Voice (Alex Performs) | Sourced Voice (Alex Sources) |
|---|---|
| "I'll be there at 6:30, set up, and calibrate for the space." | "The ensemble arrives at 6:30, fully self-contained. I handle all coordination so you don't have to." |
| "I shape the music in phases throughout the evening." | "I program the evening in phases — the musicians know exactly when to shift energy." |
| "I've performed at the Lodge many times." | "I've coordinated entertainment at venues like this for over 30 years." |
| "I read the room in real time and adjust." | "Every musician in my network understands how to read a room — that's why they're in my network." |

**The key shift:** "I do X" becomes "I ensure X happens through musicians I personally select and trust."

**What stays the same:** Validation, cinematic scenes, named fears, cultural wedges, concern resolution, close type. The five-part sequence is identical. Only the voice and transparency layer change.

#### Sourced Transparency Placement

Transparency about sourcing appears naturally in **Part 2 (Differentiator)** or **Part 3 (Concern Resolution)** of the five-part sequence. It is never the opening line and never apologetic.

**Placement pattern:**

> [Part 1 — Cinematic Hook + Validation. Paint the experience. No mention of sourcing yet.]
>
> [Part 2 — Differentiator.] "I've been curating culturally authentic entertainment in San Diego for over 30 years. I work with a small group of musicians I know personally — artists who inherited this tradition, not session players covering it."
>
> [Part 3 — Concern Resolution. Address logistics, professionalism, accountability.] "I handle all coordination — arrival, setup, sound, dress code. You have one point of contact: me."

**Why not the opening:** The client is buying an experience, not a coordination service. Lead with the experience. Introduce the curator role once they're emotionally invested in the outcome.

#### Sourced Lead Differentiator Patterns

The differentiator for sourced leads is NOT "I have a network." Every agency has a network. The differentiator is one of:

| Differentiator Type | Pattern | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Cultural authenticity** | "These aren't session musicians covering [tradition]. They grew up with this music. That authenticity is what you'll hear in the room." | Any cultural tradition lead |
| **Curation judgment** | "A full mariachi overwhelms a 40-person dinner. A 4-piece fills the room perfectly. That's the kind of call I make — and it's why the evening works." | When recommending a configuration different from what was requested |
| **Scarcity access** | "Authentic [tradition] ensembles are extremely rare in San Diego. I work with one of the only groups who specialize in this." | Bolero trio, specialty cultural traditions |
| **Single-point accountability** | "You don't manage 8 musicians. You manage me. I handle everything from booking through performance." | Large ensembles (mariachi, 5-piece), corporate clients |
| **Personal vetting** | "Every musician in my network has been vetted through years of working together. I don't dispatch strangers to your event." | When trust/professionalism is the primary concern |

#### Sourced Validation Patterns

Validation works the same way for sourced leads — it names what the CLIENT is doing, not what you're providing. But the context is slightly different because sourced leads often involve cultural significance.

| Context | Validation Line |
|---------|-----------------|
| Mariachi for parent's birthday | "You're the one making sure she hears Las Mañanitas surrounded by her family. That's not just a party — that's a gift." |
| Bolero trio for anniversary | "You decided this night deserved the real thing — not a playlist, not a cover band. That tells me something about what this anniversary means to you." |
| Cultural tradition for heritage event | "You're the one making sure [tradition] is part of this. Your family will feel it." |
| Corporate planner booking ensemble | "Your team is going to feel how much thought went into this evening." |
| Client sourcing because Alex unavailable | "This is exactly the kind of event I love coordinating. You're in good hands." |

#### Sourced Pricing Presentation

**Sourced pricing is tradition-specific, not a subcontracting fallback.** The client pays the client-facing rate regardless of delivery method. Never present sourced pricing as a markup or coordination fee.

| Wrong | Right |
|---|---|
| "The mariachi charges $1,400 and my coordination fee brings it to $1,900." | "Traditional mariachi for a 2-hour performance: $1,900." |
| "I'd need to check what the musicians charge and get back to you." | "$2,013 for two hours of authentic bolero trio." |
| "There's an additional fee because I'm coordinating rather than performing." | State the number. No breakdown. No explanation of the margin. |

**The rule:** Quote the client-facing rate with the same confidence as a solo guitar quote. The internal economics (musician cost, margin) are invisible to the client. See Rate_Card_Trio_Ensemble.md and Rate_Card_Bolero_Trio.md for sourced configuration pricing.

### The Five-Part Draft Sequence

Drafts must follow this sequence. These are not labeled sections—they flow as prose. But the order is enforced.

**Part 1 — Cinematic Hook + Validation**
Opens with the scene. The client sees themselves in it. They feel understood before they've read the second sentence. The validation lands here or immediately after—naming what THEY are doing, not what you offer.

**Part 2 — Differentiator + Named Fear**
Name what typically goes wrong with this type of booking, then show why you're different. This is not a feature list. It is one specific failure mode — the thing a lesser vendor does that this client is right to worry about — followed by the one behavior that makes you different. The fear must be named explicitly, not implied. Example: "A guitarist who shows up, plays their set at whatever volume they feel like, and never once adjusts for the room — that's the version of background music no one remembers fondly. What I do is different: I read the room in real time..." When the lead is sparse, infer the fear from context — a birthday party still implies: will the musician fit the occasion, will they be professional, will the music match the vibe. Name one explicitly.

**Sourced lead Part 2:** The named fear shifts. Instead of "a guitarist who never adjusts," the fear is "musicians who show up underprepared, play generic covers, or don't understand the cultural significance of what they're performing." The differentiator is Alex's curation: personal vetting, cultural authenticity, single-point accountability. Sourced transparency lands here naturally.

**Part 3 — Fear and Concern Resolution**
Every explicit question answered. Every implicit fear addressed. Every absent field accounted for. The client should finish this section thinking: "I don't have any more questions." No concern is left open. No detail is left for them to wonder about.

**Sourced lead Part 3:** Must include: who handles coordination (Alex), logistics (arrival, setup, sound), and accountability (single point of contact). For cultural traditions, address authenticity directly. See RESPONSE_CRAFT.md → Step 7 → Sourced Delivery Concern Patterns.

**Part 4 — Recommendation + Price**
Format, duration, configuration, dollar amount. Stated with confidence—single number, not a range (at T3). This is where the answer lands after the trust is built.

**Sourced lead Part 4:** State the configuration and price with the same confidence as a solo quote. No breakdown of musician costs. No "coordination fee" language. The price is the price for the experience. For bolero trio, consider including the 1.5-hour duration option as a natural alternative (see Bolero_Trio_Negotiation_Playbook.md).

**Part 5 — CTA**
Clear next step. One action. An invitation, not a push.

### The Validation Draft (Required Pre-Work)

**Write the validation line before the draft.**

Validation is the sentence that:
- Names what THEY are doing (not what you're providing)
- Creates ownership of the outcome
- Makes them believe this is the right choice
- Encourages action

**The psychology:** People don't buy music. They buy the feeling of having made something special happen. Validation reflects that back to them.

**Write this sentence:**
> "You're the one who [what they're doing that matters]."

**Examples by context:**

| Context | Validation Line |
|---------|-----------------|
| Pre-wedding dinner | "You're the one making sure tonight feels like more than just dinner." |
| Parent planning for child | "They're lucky to have someone who thought to do this." |
| Corporate planner | "Your team is going to feel how much thought went into this." |
| Heritage celebration | "You're the one making sure [music type] is part of it. That matters." |
| Milestone birthday | "This is the kind of detail people remember without knowing why—and you made it happen." |
| Someone who gave details | "I can tell you've thought about how you want this to feel." |
| Wedding (any role) | "You're giving your guests a moment, not just a playlist." |
| Birthday / celebration | "You decided this deserved live music. That tells me something about how you celebrate." |
| Sparse lead | "You didn't fill out much—which tells me you're still figuring it out, or you trust whoever you hire to figure it out. Either way, I've got it." |
| Mariachi for parent (sourced) | "You're the one making sure she hears Las Mañanitas surrounded by her family." |
| Bolero trio for anniversary (sourced) | "You decided this night deserved the real thing. That says everything." |
| Cultural tradition (sourced) | "You're the one making sure [tradition] is part of this celebration. Your family will feel it." |

**Then verify it's in the draft.** If you can't find explicit validation of the CLIENT (not their event, not their vision—THEM), add it.

**⚠️ Validation survives every cut.** Full draft, compressed draft, extreme competition—doesn't matter. If the response doesn't validate the client, it's incomplete. One sentence is enough. But zero is never acceptable. See Dual Output Requirement below.

**Why this closes deals:** Generic vendors describe what they offer. You reflect back what THEY'RE creating. That's the difference between "this sounds professional" and "this person gets what I'm trying to do."

### The 7-Component Checklist (Verify After Writing)

The five-part sequence above is the construction order. These seven components are the verification checklist—confirm all are present and excellent after drafting.

| # | Component | Verify It's Present |
|---|-----------|---------------------|
| 1 | **Hook** | Does the opening grab? Uses THEIR specific detail. Cinematic, not generic. |
| 2 | **Validation** | Did you affirm THEM for caring enough to plan this? (Non-negotiable) |
| 3 | **Being in the Picture** | Did you use THEIR specific details—venue, time, setting, people? |
| 4 | **Creating an Emotion** | Does the feeling emerge from the scene? (Not told—shown) |
| 5 | **Differentiation + Named Fear** | Did you name a specific failure mode a lesser vendor creates, then show why you're different? Fear must be explicit, not implied. |
| 6 | **Answer Everything** | Price, logistics, AND unasked questions they'll need answered. Absences addressed. |
| 7 | **Close** | Clear next step |

**Sourced lead additional check (#8):**

| 8 | **Sourced Integrity** | If Delivery Mode = Alex Sources: Is transparency present without being apologetic? Is Alex's curation positioned as expertise? Is accountability clear? |

### Non-Negotiables

**Validation is required, not optional.**
The client is doing something—making this event more than it had to be. Acknowledge that:
- "You're the one making sure this feels like more than just dinner."
- "They're lucky to have someone who thought to do this."
- "This is the kind of detail people remember without knowing why."

**Their details back to them.**
They said "panoramic ocean/sunset view"? Paint it. They said "both families"? Use it. They said "Torrey Pines"? You better know that patio. Generic descriptions when they gave you specifics = failure.

**Absences back to them.**
They said "not sure" on indoor/outdoor? Address it. They left budget blank? Don't ask—quote and explain. Every missing field is a signal. Use it.

**Preempt unasked questions.**
What will they need to know that they didn't ask?

| Context Clue | Preempt |
|--------------|---------|
| Outdoor / patio | Sound system, weather adaptability, battery-powered capability |
| Indoor/outdoor uncertain | Self-contained for either, adapts on the spot |
| Corporate | COI availability, professional setup |
| Wedding | Timeline flexibility, ceremony experience |
| Private residence | Self-contained, minimal footprint |
| Long format (3+ hrs) | Arc of evening (use Long-Format Expertise pattern from RESPONSE_CRAFT.md Step 7) |
| Premium venue | Venue-specific credibility claim |
| **Sourced ensemble** | Single point of contact, coordination handled, musician professionalism, cultural authenticity |
| **Large ensemble (5+)** | Space requirements, setup time, load-in logistics |
| **Mariachi** | Lead time (4-6 weeks standard, 8+ for holidays), space footprint |

### Dual Output Requirement

**Produce TWO drafts:**

**A. Full Draft**
- All components present, naturally woven
- Shows complete thinking
- Word count: 100-125 (Standard), 145-165 (Premium + Cultural)

**B. Compressed Draft**
- Send-ready, competition-calibrated
- **Minimum components (non-negotiable regardless of competition):**
  1. **Wedge** — First sentence, creates separation, references a specific detail
  2. **Validation** — One line that names what THEY'RE doing (not the event, not your service)
  3. **Fear resolution** — At least one concern addressed (explicit or inferred)
  4. **Price + logistics** — Number, key operational details
  5. **Close + contact** — Clear next step
  6. **Sourced integrity** (if applicable) — Curation credibility in one sentence

**⚠️ Validation is not optional in compressed drafts.** Competition pressure drives word count down, but validation is what creates emotional connection. A 50-word response without validation is a quote. A 55-word response with validation is a relationship. The five extra words close more deals than the five saved words ever will.

**Compressed Word Count Targets:**

| Competition | Target | Maximum |
|-------------|--------|---------|
| Low (0-3) | 100 | 125 |
| Medium (4-5) | 80 | 100 |
| High (6-10) | 60 | 80 |
| Extreme (11+) | 50 | 60 |

→ For full reference table: See QUICK_REFERENCE.md

### Qualification Responses

When Tier = Qualification (budget mismatch or vague request):

1. **Brief validation** — Acknowledge their vision without overcommitting
2. **Reframe** — Position the right solution as BETTER, not cheaper
3. **ONE strategic question** (only if low competition) — Binary, demonstrates expertise
4. **Warm indication** — Signal you'll recommend once you understand

→ See PRINCIPLES.md → "Reframe, Don't Downgrade"

### Close Types

| Type | When to Use | Pattern |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Direct Close** | Urgent, ready-to-book | "I'm available [date]—want me to lock it in?" |
| **Soft Hold** | Needs approval, planning timeline | "I can hold [date] while you finalize details." |
| **Hesitant** | Non-committal signals | "Happy to answer questions if it helps you decide." |

**Sourced close note:** For sourced leads, "I'm available" means "the ensemble is available." Frame it as: "The [configuration] is available on [date] — want me to lock it in?" or "I can hold [date] with the ensemble while you finalize."
