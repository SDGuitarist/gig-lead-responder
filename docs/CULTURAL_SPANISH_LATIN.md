## Terminology by Tradition

### Flamenco

| Term | What It Is | When to Use |
|------|------------|-------------|
| **Juerga** | A flamenco gathering where performance and participation blur | Participatory events with Spanish guests |
| **Duende** | The soul/magic—that transcendent moment when music moves the room | Elder milestones, heritage celebrations |
| **Jaleo** | Shouts of encouragement (¡Olé!, ¡Eso!) | When describing what guests will do |
| **Palmas** | Rhythmic hand clapping | Participatory events—"the palmas will come naturally" |
| **Compás** | The heartbeat rhythm everyone locks into | Rarely needed—too technical |
| **Cajón** | The box drum that adds rhythmic drive | When explaining duo vs. trio configuration |

**Usage example:**
> "With a Spanish family in the room, the palmas and jaleo will come naturally."

### Mariachi

| Term | What It Is | When to Use |
|------|------------|-------------|
| **Serenata** | A serenade—traditional romantic gesture | Proposals, anniversaries, romantic surprises |
| **Las Mañanitas** | Traditional birthday song, sung at dawn | Birthday celebrations for elders |
| **Compadres** | Close friends, like family | Describing the warmth of the gathering |

**Usage example:**
> "Las Mañanitas at her table, surrounded by three generations—that's what she'll remember."

### Bolero/Latin

| Term | What It Is | When to Use |
|------|------------|-------------|
| **Trova** | Traditional singer-songwriter style | Romantic, intimate gatherings |
| **Son** | Foundation rhythm of Cuban music | Rarely needed—too specific |

## The Juerga Dynamic

When a client requests "flamenco group" + participatory signals, they're describing a juerga—even if they don't know the word.

### Juerga Signals (Any 2+)

- "Half the room will be Spanish/European"
- "Guests will dance" (not watch)
- Two-phase event: dinner → dancing
- "Gypsy Kings style" + energy emphasis
- "Turn up the volume"

### Why Trio (Not Duo)

The dancer isn't decoration. She's the catalyst.

> "During dinner, guitar and cajón set the mood. When it's time, the dancer steps in—and with Spaniards in the house, they won't just be watching."

### Configuration Recommendations

| Event Type | Configuration | Why |
|------------|---------------|-----|
| Background/cocktail | Solo or Duo | Ambiance, not performance |
| Dinner with Spanish guests | Duo (guitar + cajón) | Present but not central |
| Participatory/juerga | Trio (+ dancer) | Dancer ignites the room |
| Performance/formal | Trio or Duo | Featured moment, guests watch |

## Painting the Room — Spanish/Latin

### Flamenco (Elder Birthday)

> "I can picture it: the guitarist begins, the cajón starts to pulse, and the music fills the room. For someone who grew up with this in their soul, being surrounded by family while flamenco plays—that's not just a party. That's being seen. That's being honored."

### Flamenco (Juerga/Participatory)

> "Dinner winds down, the dancer steps in, and suddenly the room shifts. The jaleo starts. Someone's clapping palmas. This is what your Spanish guests have been waiting for—the moment the evening becomes a juerga."

### Mariachi (Birthday for Elder)

> "Las Mañanitas at her table. Three generations surrounding her. The violins, the trumpets, the voices. She's heard this song a hundred times, but never like this—never with everyone she loves in the room."

### Mariachi (Surprise Proposal)

> "The mariachi appears. Everyone turns. She realizes what's happening before the first note finishes. That's the moment—and the music carries it."

### Spanish Guitar (Anniversary)

> "7:30. The light's softening. Twenty-five years of marriage around one table. The guitar's there in the quiet moments—not demanding attention, just holding the evening together. Someone catches your eye and raises a glass."

## Musical Bridges — Spanish/Latin

### "Classical Guitar" → Spanish/Latin

> "The warm, romantic sound you're imagining is nylon-string guitar—same instrument, different repertoire. Spanish classical and Latin boleros live on the same guitar."

### "Acoustic Guitar" + Latino Heritage

> "Acoustic guitar with Spanish and Latin repertoire will feel like home to your family—boleros, trova, the songs they grew up with."

### "Gypsy Kings Style" → Flamenco Trio

> "That driving rhythm you're hearing in your head? That's guitar plus cajón plus the energy of a dancer. A solo can't create that. The Gypsy Kings sound comes from the full ensemble."

### "Spanish Guitar" + Coastal/Wine Country Venue

> "Spanish guitar is the musical equivalent of your venue—Mediterranean warmth, California elegance. It reinforces what guests are already seeing and feeling."

## Cultural Wedge Patterns — Spanish/Latin

### For Elder Milestones

> "For someone who grew up with this music in their soul, hearing it now—surrounded by the family she built—that's not entertainment. That's being seen."
