# Bolero Trio — Negotiation Playbook

**Purpose:** Positioning language, negotiation strategy, and value framing for Bolero Trio bookings  
**Last Updated:** February 2026

**Cross-reference:** Rate_Card_Bolero_Trio.md for pricing structure

---

## Strategic Context

**The Reality:**
- 90%+ of Bolero Trio inquiries are Tier 2 (budget-conscious despite scarcity)
- Bolero trios are objectively scarce in San Diego (2-3 authentic ensembles total)
- Traditional clients covet authentic bolero programming
- They know it's rare and hard to find
- But they still need to justify the investment

**Your Positioning:**
- Scarcity + cultural significance = pricing leverage
- Not competing on price — competing on authenticity
- The emotional/value framing closes the deal, not just scarcity alone

**The Test:**
If a client specifically seeks out bolero trio (not just "any trio"), they're signaling cultural value. They'll pay premium if you frame the value correctly.

---

## The Buyer Psychology

**What they're thinking when they inquire:**
- "We want authentic bolero for [occasion]"
- "This is culturally important to us"
- "We know this is rare"
- "But we have a budget..."

**What they need from you:**
- Validation that this is the right choice
- Emotional justification to spend more than planned
- Assurance they won't find this elsewhere
- Permission to say yes

**Your job:** Make them feel great about the investment, not guilty about the price.

---

## The Anchor Conversation

**When quoting T2D 2hrs at $2,013:**

> "For authentic bolero trio programming, the investment is $2,013 for two hours. This includes [guitarist name], [requinto player], and [vocalist] — they're one of the only authentic bolero ensembles in San Diego who specialize in traditional Mexican romantic repertoire."

**Deliver with confidence, not apology.** The price is the price because the product is rare.

**They'll likely respond:**
- "We need to think about it" → **NORMAL, EXPECTED**
- "Can I discuss with [partner/family]?" → **GOOD SIGN**
- "That's more than we planned..." → **NEGOTIATION BEGINS**

**Your response to "we need to think about it":**

> "Absolutely. This is a significant investment and you should feel great about the decision. I'll send you the formal quote. Take your time, and let me know if any questions come up."

**Then stop talking.** Don't oversell. Let them process.

---

## Negotiation Sequence (In Order)

### Stage 1: They Come Back Pushing Back

**What they'll say:**
- "We really want this, but our budget is tight..."
- "Is there any flexibility on the price?"
- "We love the idea, but $2,000 is more than we planned..."

**What NOT to say:**
- "Let me check with the musicians..."
- "I can probably work something out..."
- "How much were you thinking?"

**What to say instead (Duration Lever):**

> "I completely understand. What if we structured this as 90 minutes instead of 2 hours? That brings it to **$1,520**, and you still get the authentic bolero experience during [key moment — toast, first dance, dinner service]."

**Why this works:**
- Drops price by ~25% ($2,013 → $1,520)
- Feels like smart negotiating, not begging for discount
- You're right-sizing the service, not discounting quality
- They get the cultural experience they want

**Expected outcome:** ~70% accept duration adjustment immediately.

---

### Stage 2: They're Still Hesitant After Duration Offer

**If they say:**
- "Even $1,520 is a stretch..."
- "We were hoping for closer to $1,200-1,400..."
- "Is there any other way to make this work?"

**Your response:**

> "Let me reach out to the ensemble and see if there's any flexibility on their end for [date/occasion]. I'll get back to you within 24 hours."

**Then contact the trio.** Ask if they can reduce their rate for this booking.

---

### Stage 3A: Trio Agrees to Flex

**When trio agrees to reduce their rate:**

> "Good news — I was able to work with the ensemble and we can make this happen at **$1,750** for the full 2 hours."

*OR*

> "Good news — I was able to work with the ensemble and we can make this happen at **$1,650** for the full 2 hours."

**Deliver with enthusiasm.** You went to bat for them, and it worked.

**Expected outcome:** ~80% book at this price.

---

### Stage 3B: Trio Won't Flex

**When trio holds firm on their rate:**

> "I checked with the ensemble, and unfortunately their rates are set for this season. The absolute best I can do is **$1,850** for 2 hours. That's below my standard rate, but I really want to make this work for [occasion/anniversary/celebration]."

**Hold this line.** This is your floor when supplier won't flex.

**Expected outcome:** ~30% accept, ~70% admit they can't afford it.

---

### Stage 4: Hold Firm or Walk Away

**If they push below your floor ($1,850 when trio won't flex, or $1,650-1,750 when trio does flex):**

> "I completely understand budget constraints. I wish I could make the numbers work lower than that, but at that price point I wouldn't be able to deliver the authentic bolero trio experience you deserve. I'd rather recommend an alternative than compromise on the cultural authenticity that makes this special."

**Then gracefully exit:**

> "If you'd like, I can recommend [competitor name] who might have different pricing, or we could explore [solo Spanish guitar / Latin duo / flamenco duo] which would give you beautiful romantic atmosphere at a different investment level."

**What happens:**
- They either find the money and book → Great
- They admit they can't afford it → **They weren't real buyers anyway**
- They walk away → **Fine, you weren't going to make acceptable margin**

## The Value Framing Language

**Use this when they're on the fence:**

### Scarcity Lever

> "Authentic bolero trios are extremely rare in San Diego. We're talking about one of maybe two ensembles who actually specialize in traditional bolero repertoire — not generic Latin musicians covering boleros, but musicians who grew up with this music as part of their cultural heritage."

### Emotional Lever

> "This isn't background music. Every client who books bolero trio tells me it was the most memorable part of their event. Your guests will be talking about it for years. That's what you're investing in — not just music, but an authentic cultural experience that very few people get to have."

### Cultural Authenticity Lever

> "Traditional bolero trio requires requinto guitar — that's an entirely different instrument from standard guitar, and authentic requinto playing is extremely rare. These musicians didn't learn this from YouTube; they inherited this tradition. That authenticity is what makes the experience powerful."

### The Close (After Value Framing)

> "For [anniversary/parent's celebration/cultural milestone], this is the investment that turns a nice event into an unforgettable one. That's what you're paying for — a memory that lasts."

**Then stop talking.** Let them make the decision.

---

## The Timing Flexibility Option

**If budget is truly tight but they want the experience:**

> "Is your date flexible at all? If we could move to [weekday/off-peak time], I might be able to offer better pricing because the musicians have more availability."

**Use this sparingly.** Only when:
- You genuinely want the booking
- Date change is actually possible
- You're willing to quote $100-200 less for off-peak

**Why it works:** Feels like collaboration, not desperation.

---

## The Bundle Strategy

**If they need multiple services:**

> "Are you still looking for ceremony music? What if we bundled bolero trio for your reception with solo Spanish guitar for your ceremony? I could do **$2,800 total** instead of pricing them separately."

**When to use:**
- They mention needing other entertainment
- Total event budget exists
- Bundling increases total revenue even if per-service margin is lower

**The psychology:** Bundling feels like smart planning, not upselling.

---

## When to Walk Away

**Walk away gracefully when:**
- They won't meet your floor after supplier flex attempt
- They keep asking for "just a little more" discount after you've moved twice
- They compare you to commodity trio pricing ($1,200-1,400 range)
- They say "we found someone else for $1,000" — they're not comparing apples to apples

**The graceful exit:**

> "I really appreciate you considering us. At that price point, you're likely looking at a different type of ensemble than what we're offering. I want you to have authentic bolero programming for [occasion], and I'd rather you work with someone who can deliver that at your budget than for me to compromise on quality."

**Why this works:**
- You maintain positioning (not desperate)
- You plant seed of doubt about cheaper option
- They might come back when they realize cheap option isn't authentic
- If they don't come back, **they weren't your client anyway**

---

## The "Not a Real Buyer" Signals

**These indicate price shopping, not culture shopping:**
- "Someone quoted us $1,200 for bolero trio" (probably not authentic)
- "Can't you just do 2 musicians instead of 3?" (doesn't understand the format)
- "We just want background music" (missing the cultural value)
- Endless negotiation after you've moved price twice (never satisfied)

**When you see these signals:** Walk away. They're not buying what you're selling.

---

## The 1.5-Hour Compromise Script

**When offering duration adjustment:**

> "What if we did this: 90 minutes of bolero trio during the heart of your [reception/dinner/celebration]. That's the featured performance during [specific moment], and we bring it to **$1,520**. You get the authentic experience without the full 2-hour commitment."

**Why 1.5 hours works:**
- Still delivers cultural moment
- Significant price drop (~25%)
- Feels like right-sizing, not discounting
- Easy to structure (show up at 7:00, play until 8:30, done)

**Position it as:** Smart event design, not budget constraint.

---

## Post-Negotiation Close

**Once they agree to any price:**

> "Perfect. I'm excited to make this happen for you. Bolero trio programming is really special, and I know your [guests/family/honoree] are going to remember this moment. Let me get the contract over to you today."

**Then move fast:**
- Send contract within 2 hours
- Get deposit within 24 hours
- Lock it in before they second-guess

**Why speed matters:** Buyer's remorse is real. Fast execution prevents cold feet.

## Expected Booking Funnel

**Out of 100 Bolero Trio Inquiries:**
- **40-50** accept anchor price immediately ($2,013 for 2hrs)
- **35-42** accept after duration adjustment ($1,520 for 1.5hrs)
- **4-7** book after supplier flex ($1,650-1,750 for 2hrs)
- **3-4** book at floor when supplier won't flex ($1,850 for 2hrs)
- **5-15** walk away (price shoppers, not real buyers)

**Conversion rate: 85-95%** of genuine bolero trio inquiries convert to bookings.

**Key insight:** The 5-15% who walk away weren't your clients. They wanted commodity trio pricing for specialty programming. Let them go.

## Strategic Reminders

**This is scarcity pricing, not commodity pricing.**
- You're offering something genuinely rare
- Authentic bolero trios are hard to find
- Cultural value justifies premium over generic trio

**T2 clients need emotional justification, not just price.**
- They have budget constraints
- But they'll find the money if they feel great about it
- Your job is to validate the decision, not defend the price

**Duration adjustment is your best negotiation lever.**
- Drops price significantly (~25%)
- Delivers the experience they want
- Doesn't feel like discounting

**Supplier flexibility gives you negotiation room.**
- Ask trio to flex when client is serious but stuck
- They'll flex ~30-50% of the time
- When they do, client usually books
- When they don't, you hold firm and client self-qualifies

**Walk away from price shoppers.**
- If they won't meet floor, they're not buying what you're selling
- Graceful exit protects positioning
- They might come back when cheap option disappoints
- If they don't, you avoided low-margin hassle
